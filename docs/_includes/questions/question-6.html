#6 - Study of the use of force by police officers

In this part we analyze how police officers' use of force varies depending on the skin colour of the subject of a police stop. In order to study this variable we focused on the police district of San Jose (California) which was the sole district describing the use of force by police officers during a police stop. We had to limit ourselves to a global study of the use of force by police officers by the whole department rather than studying individual police officers racial bias for the use of force since officers id were not indicated in the data which impeached us linking arrests together.

Use of force

After cleaning up the dataset we found the following force types were described: The fact that someone was handcuffed, "no curb", making someone sit in the police vehicle. We dropped the "no curb" because we could not find out what it meant. We set that force was used either when someone was handcuffed, or when someone was ordered to sit into the police vehicle.

There exists a difference between how much use of force is applied between races. Black people and hispanic are the ones that are most subject to force. For black persons a recorded use of force appears 11.3% of the time during a police stop. This is higher than the 7.7% of white people and the 5% for people that are asian/pacific islander. Also for hispanic and black people a bigger number of police stops does not even describe if police force was used or not. It is hard to describe if it means anything.

Another point which caught our eye in this plot is that people who are not assimilated to any race are significantly more often subject to any use of force. Is not describing a subject race when force is actually used a way to hide the usage of force on a minority?


Reason for force use

We now focus on the times when force is used in order to describe what are the reasons for using force on different populations.

The reason which may seem the most reasonable to use force for would be in our sense when someone is armed with a weapon. This reason is 1.7 more prevalent for black people compared to other population. For hispanic people it is 1,54 more prevalent than for white people. But one has to remember that the numbers are pretty low here.

Officer safety seems to be a pretty vague reason to use force for. There is a difference of 10% between the proportion of black people to which force is applied and the proportion to which force is applied on asian people. The "other" category for subject race seems to appear less in this "officer safety" reason.

Another vague reason is the "not described" reason. This time "not described" is twice more prevalent for people that have been assigned "other" as a subject race than for hispanic and black persons. But also this vague reason is also assigned more often to white and asian ethnicities which have numbers closer to the "other" category.
